How the peg pyramid works

The board is built as a triangle of staggered pegs. Each row is wider than the one above it, so a ball has a choice to fall left or right at every step. After crossing the full set of rows, it settles into one of the pockets along the base, with one more pocket than there are rows.

Because each bounce acts like a simple coin flip, most balls end up near the middle and only a few reach the outer edges. That is why the center pockets pay less and the side pockets can pay much more. For players using bonus kong betting options, the shape of the board is what creates the balance between frequent small returns and rare larger hits.

Six words worth knowing

Pocket
A slot at the bottom of the board that holds the multiplier for the final landing point.
Rows
The number of peg levels a ball crosses; more rows create more pockets and a sharper curve.
Risk level
A preset that changes how the multipliers are spread without changing the basic return much.

The small gap between the true odds and the paid odds that supports the operator.

A system that lets players check that the result was fixed before the ball moved.
Variance
The scale of the swings around the average result, which is what the risk setting really changes.
